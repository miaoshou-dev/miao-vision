# Building AI Products That Users Trust

Source: https://example.com/ai-trust-interview
Subtitle: Industry leaders share their perspectives on responsible AI product development.

> "Trust is not a feature you can ship in a sprint. It is earned over time through consistent behavior and transparent communication."

This was the opening remark from Dr. Sarah Chen, VP of AI Products at a major cloud provider, during our roundtable on trustworthy AI. She highlighted that organizations often treat trust as a compliance checkbox rather than a product attribute.

> "The biggest mistake I see is teams shipping AI features without explaining what the system can and cannot do. Users fill the gap with assumptions, and those assumptions are usually wrong."

Product leader Marcus Rivera emphasized the importance of clear capability boundaries. His team publishes model cards and behavior examples alongside every AI feature release.

> "We stopped measuring AI success by accuracy alone. If the model is 99% accurate but users don't understand when it fails, that's a product failure."

Dr. Aisha Patel, an AI ethics researcher, pointed out that user understanding of AI systems is often lower than teams assume. She recommends investing in UX patterns that communicate uncertainty.

> "The teams that do this well treat AI as a collaborator, not an oracle. They design the experience around the human decision-maker."

The roundtable participants agreed on three foundational practices. First, publish clear capability documentation. Second, invest in feedback loops that capture when users override AI suggestions. Third, measure user trust explicitly through surveys and behavioral signals.

> "If you wait until launch to think about trust, it is already too late. Trust needs to be designed into the product from day one."

Engineers, product managers, and executives all have a role to play in building trustworthy AI products. The consensus was clear: trust is a cross-functional responsibility, not a solo team's concern.
